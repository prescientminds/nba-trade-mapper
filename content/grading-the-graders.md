# Grading the Graders: Kevin Pelton's ESPN Trade Grades vs. Reality

**By Mara Kingsley** | Verdict Revisit

---

Kevin Pelton has been ESPN's sole trade grader since at least 2013. Every deadline and offseason, he assigns letter grades to each side of every major NBA trade. The column runs, people react, and then everyone moves on.

We went back. We took 24 Pelton trade grades confirmed from 2016–2021 — recovered from ABC News mirrors, local sports sites, and forum quotes — and ran them against 4-5 years of actual player production. The CATV scoring system counts win shares, playoff performance, championships, and accolades. It doesn't care about draft position, contract structure, or name recognition.

Pelton's record on 26 evaluable winner calls: **11 correct, 15 wrong. 42% accuracy.**

That's worse than a coin flip.

---

## The Five Worst Misses

### 1. Celtics-76ers Pick Swap (2017) — ESPN: PHI B, BOS B → Reality: BOS A (95.6), PHI F (0.5)

The largest CATV gap in the entire dataset. Pelton called this a dead-even trade. Boston moved down from 1 to 3 and got Jayson Tatum — a franchise player, MVP candidate, 5x All-Star, 95.6 CATV. Philadelphia moved up to 1 and got Markelle Fultz — who forgot how to shoot, produced 0.5 CATV, and was traded within two years.

The gap between Pelton's prediction and reality: 95.1 CATV. No other miss is in the same zip code.

**Pelton's accuracy: F**

---

### 2. IT/Clarkson Swap — Lakers-Cavs (2018) — ESPN: LAL A, CLE D → Reality: CLE B- (18.1), LAL F (0.5)

Pelton gave the Lakers an A for Isaiah Thomas. Thomas was physically broken. He played 17 games in a Lakers jersey, was waived, and produced 0.0 CATV. The 2018 first became Moritz Wagner (0.2). Channing Frye added 0.3.

Cleveland got Larry Nance Jr. — 14.8 CATV over 4+ years as a rotation big — and Jordan Clarkson (3.3). Combined: 18.1. The Lakers' combined return: 0.5.

**ESPN gave LAL an A. Actual grade: F.**

**Pelton's accuracy: F**

---

### 3. CP3 to PHX (2020) — ESPN: OKC A, PHX B- → Reality: PHX B+ (34.9), OKC D (1.8)

Pelton gave OKC an A for Ricky Rubio (waived), Kelly Oubre (flipped immediately), Ty Jerome (1.8 total CATV), and a first-round pick. OKC's entire A-graded return: 1.8 CATV.

Phoenix got a player who took them from lottery to NBA Finals in year one, then to 64 wins in year two. CP3 produced 34.2 CATV. Pelton was skeptical of a 35-year-old on a big contract. CP3 made the Finals.

**Pelton's accuracy: F**

---

### 4. Harris to PHI (2019) — ESPN: LAC A, PHI C- → Reality: PHI A- (44.6), LAC C- (4.0)

Pelton gave the Clippers an A for Shamet, two late firsts, Muscala, and Chandler. Total LAC return: 4.0 CATV. He gave Philadelphia a C- for Tobias Harris — whose max contract was widely mocked.

Harris produced 40.0 CATV over 5+ seasons. The C- for a player who became the most productive asset in a 44.6 CATV haul. Pelton graded the contract, not the player.

**Pelton's accuracy: F**

---

### 5. Butler to MIN (2017) — ESPN: MIN A, CHI F → Reality: CHI A- (46.8), MIN C+ (11.7)

The only F Pelton ever gave. He gave it to Chicago — for a package that included a 22-year-old Zach LaVine (31.1 CATV, 2x All-Star), a rookie Lauri Markkanen (11.8, future All-NBA), and Kris Dunn (3.8). Total: 46.8 CATV. A- value.

Butler gave Minnesota 2 seasons, 1 playoff appearance, then demanded a trade. 11.7 CATV.

The only F in Pelton's history, and it was wrong by 8 grades.

**Pelton's accuracy: F**

## The Five Best Calls

### 1. Westbrook to LAL (2021) — ESPN: LAL D → Reality: LAL D (2.4)

Pelton saw the disaster coming. Westbrook produced 2.4 CATV and was traded after one season. The grade matched reality exactly. The caveat: everyone saw this one coming.

### 2. Nurkic-Plumlee Swap (2017) — ESPN: POR B, DEN C- → Reality: POR B (25.4), DEN B- (16.0)

The cleanest structural call in the dataset. Pelton got the winner right, the relative margin right, and Portland's grade exactly right. Nurkic became Portland's franchise center. Plumlee was a solid starter. Both sides got value — Portland got more. Pelton saw it.

### 3. Bledsoe to MIL (2017) — ESPN: MIL B-, PHX C → Reality: MIL B (20.9), PHX D (1.4)

Right winner, conservative grades, real production. Bledsoe gave Milwaukee 3 seasons including a championship contribution. One of the few trades where Pelton was conservative rather than inflated — and it aged well.

### 4. Ibaka to TOR (2017) — ESPN: TOR A-, ORL B → Reality: TOR B (21.3), ORL C+ (10.2)

Right winner. Ibaka gave Toronto 4+ years of production across multiple playoff runs. Pelton's best instinct: trusting the established player over the younger asset when the established player commits long-term.

### 5. Bradley-Morris Swap (2017) — ESPN: BOS B, DET D → Reality: BOS C+ (9.8), DET F (-0.2)

Right winner, right direction. Pelton's D for Detroit was prescient — Bradley left in free agency after one season and produced negative CATV. Morris gave Boston 2+ solid years.

## The Patterns

### 1. Grade Inflation

Pelton's A grades correspond to C+ actual value on average. Across all confirmed A and A- grades, the median CATV score is 8.5 — which maps to C+ on the CATV scale. His top grades are overrated by roughly 5 grades.

The Clippers got an A for Shamet (4.0 CATV). OKC got an A for Jerome + salary filler (1.8). The Lakers got an A for a broken Isaiah Thomas (0.5). Brooklyn got an A for Jared Dudley (1.8). Cleveland got an A for IT + Crowder + the 8th pick (7.1).

Pelton's A means "I think this team had a good day." It does not predict production.

### 2. The Young Player Discount

The most consistent bias: undervaluing young players in trade packages.

- LaVine + Markkanen + Dunn (all under 23) → **F**. Produced 46.8 CATV.
- Buddy Hield → **D**. Produced 15.6 CATV.
- Larry Nance Jr. + Jordan Clarkson → **D**. Produced 18.1 CATV.
- Tony Snell → **C-**. Produced 9.6 CATV.
- Jayson Tatum (as No. 3 pick) → **B**. Produced 95.6 CATV.

Young players in trade packages are structurally undergraded because their value is speculative at the moment of the trade. Pelton consistently resolves that uncertainty downward.

### 3. The Rental Trap

Pelton overvalues the team acquiring the established star, especially when the star is a rental who leaves in free agency.

- Butler left Minnesota after 2 seasons → Pelton graded MIN as the winner (A).
- CP3 was traded from OKC → Pelton graded OKC as the winner (A).
- Cousins tore his Achilles and left NOP → Pelton graded NOP as the winner (A-).
- Russell was traded from Brooklyn within 2 years → Pelton graded BKN as the winner (A-).

The pattern: the team that gets the bigger name gets the bigger grade, regardless of how long that name stays.

### 4. Reputation Grading

Isaiah Thomas had been an MVP candidate 8 months before the Lakers traded for him. He was physically destroyed. Pelton graded him an A. Blake Griffin had a max contract with injury risk. Pelton gave Detroit a D. Tobias Harris was a "nice player" without star cachet. Pelton gave Philly a C- for him — he produced 40.0 CATV on the max.

Pelton's grade correlates with the player's reputation at the moment of the trade, not with what they're likely to produce going forward.

### 5. The Afterthought Problem

The most valuable player in the Harden 4-team trade was Jarrett Allen — a throw-in (53.1 CATV). The most productive asset in the Russell-Lopez swap was Kyle Kuzma — the 27th pick (13.2). Larry Nance Jr. was the lesser name in the IT/Clarkson swap — he produced 14.8 over 4+ years. Jayson Tatum was the third pick, not the first — he became an MVP candidate.

Trade graders focus on the headliner. CATV counts everyone.

---

## Pelton's Overall Grade: C-

He gets the winner right 42% of the time. His A grades predict C+ value. His only F was wrong by 8 grades. His best calls (Westbrook, Nurkic, Bledsoe, Ibaka) show he has genuine basketball instincts — but they emerge most clearly on smaller trades where reputation noise is lower. On blockbusters, the bias toward the bigger name overwhelms the analysis.

The defense: trade grading at the moment of the trade is structurally hard. Injuries, free agency departures, and draft busts are unpredictable. Fair. But Pelton's errors aren't random — they're systematically biased toward star names, against young assets, and inflated by 5 grades at the top. A random grader with no bias would hit 50% on winner calls. Pelton hits 42%.
