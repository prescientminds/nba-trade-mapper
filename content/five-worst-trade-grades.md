# The Five Worst Trade Grades Ever Published

**By Mara Kingsley** | Verdict Revisit

---

Every major NBA trade gets graded within hours. ESPN, Sports Illustrated, CBS, Bleacher Report — the column drops, the takes fly, and everyone moves on. Nobody goes back.

We did. We pulled every confirmed trade grade we could find from 2016 through 2021, then ran each trade against 4-5 years of actual player production using our CATV scoring system. Win shares, playoff performance, championships, accolades. The model doesn't care about reputation or contract structure. It counts what happened.

These are the five worst individual trade grades ever published — cases where an outlet was not just wrong about who won, but dramatically, structurally wrong about what a team was getting. We excluded trades where the miss was primarily driven by a major injury. These are judgment calls. The experts looked at the players, evaluated the assets, and got it backwards.

---

## 5. ESPN gave OKC an A for the Chris Paul trade. OKC's entire return produced 1.8 CATV.

**Trade:** Phoenix sent Ricky Rubio, Kelly Oubre Jr., Ty Jerome, Jalen Lecque, and a protected 2022 first-round pick to Oklahoma City. OKC sent Chris Paul and Abdel Nader to Phoenix.

**The grade:** OKC: **A** | PHX: **B-** — Kevin Pelton, ESPN
**The reality:** PHX **B+** (34.9 CATV) | OKC **D** (1.8 CATV)

Chris Paul was 35 years old on a $41 million contract. Pelton's thesis: Phoenix took on the risk, OKC shed the salary and stockpiled assets for their rebuild. The Thunder got the better end.

Here's what Oklahoma City actually got. Ricky Rubio never played a game for them — he was rerouted to Minnesota. Kelly Oubre Jr. was flipped to Golden State within weeks. Jalen Lecque was waived and never played an NBA game again. The protected first-round pick hasn't conveyed as of this writing. Ty Jerome — the only player who stuck around — produced 1.8 CATV across his career. That's the entire return. 1.8.

Chris Paul took a Suns team that went 34-39 the year before and led them to the NBA Finals. Phoenix won 51 games in his first season. Then 64 in his second. CP3 produced 34.2 CATV — 19 times what OKC's entire A-graded haul produced. He made two All-Star teams, finished 4th in MVP voting, and turned Devin Booker from a losing-team scorer into a winning-team star.

Pelton was betting on age decline. CP3 didn't decline. The grade was built on an actuarial table, not a basketball evaluation.

**Grade gap:** 8 grades (A → D)

## 4. Sports Illustrated gave Cleveland an A+ for the Kyrie Irving return. The Brooklyn pick landed at 8.

**Trade:** Boston sent Isaiah Thomas, Jae Crowder, Ante Zizic, and the Brooklyn Nets' 2018 first-round pick to Cleveland. Cleveland sent Kyrie Irving to Boston.

**The grade:** CLE: **A+** | BOS: **B** — Rohan Nadkarni, Sports Illustrated
**Also:** CLE: **A** | BOS: **C-** — Hoops Habit; CLE: **A** | BOS: **C** — Kevin Pelton, ESPN
**The reality:** BOS **B** (20.4 CATV) | CLE **C** (7.1 CATV)

The Brooklyn pick was the centerpiece. The Nets had been one of the worst teams in the league for years. Every projection had their 2018 pick landing in the top 3 of what was supposed to be a loaded draft — Luka Doncic, DeAndre Ayton, Marvin Bagley. The A+ was built on the assumption that Cleveland was getting a franchise player at the top of that draft.

The Nets improved. The pick landed at 8. Cleveland selected Collin Sexton — a decent scoring guard who averaged 20 a game one season, then tore his meniscus and was traded to Utah on a sign-and-trade within four years. His CATV: 5.5. Not a franchise cornerstone. Not close.

Isaiah Thomas played 32 games for Cleveland before being shipped out. His hip was already compromised before the trade — the injury was public knowledge, and Pelton even revised his initial grade down once the hip report surfaced. But every outlet still gave Cleveland the edge. Jae Crowder was a solid role player who lasted months. Ante Zizic never cracked a rotation. The entire A+-graded package: 7.1 CATV.

Kyrie Irving gave Boston two productive seasons — 20.4 CATV — including a run to Game 7 of the 2018 Eastern Conference Finals against LeBron's Cavaliers. He was difficult, he left, but he produced while he was there.

The miss: everyone bet the entire grade on the Brooklyn pick being top-3. When it wasn't, there was nothing left.

**Grade gap:** 7 grades (A+ → C)

## 3. ESPN gave the Clippers an A for the Tobias Harris return. The whole package produced 4.0 CATV.

**Trade:** The Clippers sent Tobias Harris, Mike Scott, and Boban Marjanovic to Philadelphia. Philadelphia sent Landry Shamet, Wilson Chandler, Mike Muscala, a 2020 first-round pick, and a 2021 first-round pick to the Clippers.

**The grade:** LAC: **A** | PHI: **C-** — Kevin Pelton, ESPN
**Also:** LAC: **A-** | PHI: **A-** — Sports Illustrated
**The reality:** PHI **A-** (44.6 CATV) | LAC **C-** (4.0 CATV)

The Clippers got five assets for one player. On paper, it looked like a heist — a young shooter in Shamet, two first-round picks, and salary filler. Pelton graded it an A. The logic: Harris was a nice player but not a max player, and the Clippers extracted maximum value before he walked.

Harris didn't walk. He signed a five-year, $180 million max extension with Philadelphia. Everyone mocked it. He then played five full seasons: 19 points, 7 rebounds a night, available, productive, steady. He produced 40.0 CATV. Mike Scott added another 4.7 as a rotation piece. Philadelphia's total: 44.6.

The Clippers' return: Landry Shamet averaged 9 points a game for LA, was traded to Brooklyn after one season, bounced to Phoenix, then to Portland. His CATV: 4.2. Wilson Chandler produced -0.1. Mike Muscala produced 0.0. The 2020 first-round pick became Luke Kennard (not included in CATV as he was drafted elsewhere). The 2021 first became a late pick. Total Clippers return: 4.0 CATV.

Pelton gave the Clippers an A for C- production and Philadelphia a C- for A- production. The grades were exactly inverted. Harris wasn't exciting. He was the most quietly productive player in this entire dataset — 40.0 CATV on the contract everyone laughed at.

**Grade gap:** 7 grades (A → C-)

## 2. Every outlet called Philadelphia the winner of the Celtics pick swap. Boston got Jayson Tatum.

**Trade:** Boston sent the No. 1 overall pick in the 2017 draft to Philadelphia. Philadelphia sent the No. 3 overall pick and a future Los Angeles Lakers first-round pick to Boston. Philadelphia selected Markelle Fultz at 1. Boston selected Jayson Tatum at 3.

**The grades:** PHI: **B** | BOS: **B** — Kevin Pelton, ESPN. Sports Illustrated headline: "Grades: Sixers Best Celtics in Win-Win Draft Trade."
**The reality:** BOS **A** (95.6 CATV) | PHI **F** (0.5 CATV)

Markelle Fultz was the consensus No. 1 pick. He'd averaged 23 points a game at Washington. He could score at all three levels. Every mock draft had him first. Danny Ainge looked at that consensus and traded the pick. He moved down from 1 to 3, selected Jayson Tatum — a one-and-done from Duke who most boards had third — and pocketed a future Lakers first-round pick as the kicker.

Every outlet treated this as Philadelphia winning, or at best a wash. SI's headline said the Sixers "bested" the Celtics. Pelton graded both sides B. The logic: the gap between the No. 1 pick and No. 3 pick was larger than the value of the extra first-rounder Boston received.

Tatum became a five-time All-Star, a two-time All-NBA First Team selection, an MVP candidate who finished top-5 in voting, and the best player on the 2024 championship Celtics. He has produced 95.6 CATV — the highest single-asset score in our entire dataset.

Fultz's jumper looked wrong from Summer League. The mechanics had changed — the fluid pull-up he'd shown at Washington was gone, replaced by a hitchy, uncertain motion. He shot 33% from the field in his first 14 games. He was shut down, eventually diagnosed with thoracic outlet syndrome, and traded to Orlando after one partial season. His career CATV: 0.5.

The gap between what Pelton predicted (even trade) and what happened (95.6 vs. 0.5) is 95.1 CATV. No other miss in the dataset is within 50 points of it. Ainge told everyone exactly what he thought by trading the pick. Every grader watched him do it and still said Philadelphia won.

**Grade gap:** 95.1 CATV. The largest prediction-to-reality gap in the dataset.

## 1. ESPN gave Chicago an F — the only F he's ever given — for Zach LaVine, Lauri Markkanen, and Kris Dunn. They produced an A-.

**Trade:** Chicago sent Jimmy Butler and the 16th pick in the 2017 draft to Minnesota. Minnesota sent Zach LaVine, Kris Dunn, and the 7th pick in the 2017 draft to Chicago. Chicago selected Lauri Markkanen with the 7th pick.

**The grade:** CHI: **F** | MIN: **A** — Kevin Pelton, ESPN
**Also:** CHI: **D** | MIN: **A** — Rob Mahoney, Sports Illustrated; CHI: **D** | MIN: **A-** — Hoops Habit
**The reality:** CHI **A-** (46.8 CATV) | MIN **C+** (11.7 CATV)

Kevin Pelton has published hundreds of trade grades across a decade. He has given exactly one F. He gave it to Chicago for this trade. Sports Illustrated and Hoops Habit gave D's. No outlet gave Chicago higher than a D.

Here's what Chicago got.

Zach LaVine was 22 years old and coming off a torn ACL. He'd averaged 19 points a game for Minnesota before the injury. The question was whether his athleticism would come back. It did. LaVine became a two-time All-Star, averaged 25 points a game across multiple seasons, and produced 31.1 CATV — more than Butler produced for Minnesota, Philadelphia, and Miami combined over the same window.

Lauri Markkanen was a 7-foot Finnish forward out of Arizona, selected 7th overall with the pick Chicago received in the deal. He put up 15 and 7 as a rookie, spent three seasons in Chicago (11.8 CATV), was traded to Cleveland, then to Utah, where he averaged 26 points a game and made the All-NBA Second Team.

Kris Dunn was a point guard who'd struggled as a rookie in Minnesota. He gave Chicago two and a half productive seasons as a defender and secondary ball-handler — 3.8 CATV — before leaving in free agency.

Combined package: 46.8 CATV. That's A- value.

Here's what Minnesota got.

Jimmy Butler played two seasons for the Timberwolves. In his first year, he led them to their first playoff appearance since 2004 — a five-game loss to Houston in the first round. In his second year, he grew unhappy with the organization, the young players (Karl-Anthony Towns and Andrew Wiggins), and the direction of the team. He held out of training camp. He showed up to a practice, joined the third-stringers, beat the starters, and reportedly told the front office: "You f---ing need me." He was traded to Philadelphia in November. His total CATV for Minnesota: 11.7. C+.

Pelton's F was built on a defensible premise — Butler was a top-15 player, and LaVine (coming off an ACL), Dunn (after a bad rookie year), and the 7th pick didn't look like adequate return for a player of that caliber. Every other outlet agreed. The premise was correct. Butler was a top-15 player.

But the F didn't account for the most important variable in any trade: how long the acquired player will play for you. Butler played two seasons. LaVine played six. Markkanen played three in Chicago and is still producing elsewhere. Duration compounds. Stars who leave are rentals. Young players who stay are assets.

Every outlet missed this in the same direction. The consensus was unanimous. The consensus was wrong by 35.1 CATV.

**Grade gap:** 8 grades (F → A-). The only F ever assigned by ESPN's trade grader, aimed at the team that won the trade.

---

*Methodology: CATV = Win Shares + Playoff WS × 1.5 + Championship Contribution + Accolade Bonuses. Only post-trade production on the acquiring team counts. Minimum 4 seasons of hindsight. Grade scale: A+ (100+), A (60+), A- (45+), B+ (30+), B (20+), B- (12+), C+ (8+), C (5+), C- (3+), D (1+), F (0). Full data at NBA Trade Mapper.*
